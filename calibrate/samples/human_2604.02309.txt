abstract
 Doubly stochastic matrices enable learned mixing across residual streams, but parameterizing the set of doubly stochastic matrices (the Birkhoff polytope) exactly and efficiently remains an open challenge. Existing exact methods scale factorially with the number of streams ( d ), while Kronecker-factorized approaches are efficient but expressivity-limited. We introduce a novel exact parameterization grounded in the theory of generalized orthostochastic matrices, which scales as O(d^3) and exposes a single hyperparameter s which continuously interpolates between a computationally efficient boundary and the fully expressive Birkhoff polytope. Building on Manifold-Constrained Hyper-Connections ( ), a framework for learned dynamic layer connectivity, we instantiate this parameterization in go- . Our method composes naturally with Kronecker-factorized methods, substantially recovering expressivity at similar FLOP costs. Spectral analysis indicates that go- \ fills the Birkhoff polytope far more completely than Kronecker-factorized baselines. On synthetic stream-mixing tasks, go- \ achieves the minimum theoretical loss while converging up to 10 faster. We validate our approach in a 30M parameter GPT-style language model. The expressivity, efficiency, and exactness of go- \ offer a practical avenue for scaling d as a new dimension of model capacity.

Introduction

The performance and stability of modern machine learning models increasingly depends on the geometric structure of their underlying matrix parameterizations [citation]. While traditional constraints—such as symmetry or orthogonality—have long served as foundational tools [citation], recent breakthroughs have pivoted toward more complex geometric structures [citation].
A promising frontier for addressing these limitations lies in leveraging manifold theory to map unconstrained optimization spaces onto computationally tractable, constrained geometries [citation]. Of particular interest is the Birkhoff polytope [citation], the manifold of doubly stochastic matrices. By enforcing unit row and column sum constraints, the Birkhoff polytope offers a rigorous framework for learning permutations, alignments, and hyper-connections, yet its practical utility has been bottlenecked by the trade-off between exactness and computational scalability [citation].

[citation] demonstrated that constraining the attention pattern to doubly stochastic matrices both improved accuracy in vision and language models while providing an elegant interpretation of learned parameters as probability trajectories [citation]. [citation] investigated memory-efficient implementations of Sinkhorn Attention due to it's ability to mix internal representations and have an associated smooth optimization landscape.

A more recent application of such manifold constraints is in the generalization of residual connections [citation].
Residual connections have become a crucial component in stabilizing the training of deep neural networks [citation] and a critical building block of modern large language models [citation].
Ongoing work investigates dynamic generalizations of the residual streams aimed at increasing the topological complexity of residual function connectivity by increasing the number of streams, d , while preserving the identity mapping property of the residual stream [citation].

Manifold-Constrained Hyper-Connections ( ) [citation] provides evidence that constraining the residual connections to the interior of the Birkhoff polytope significantly improves training stability and scalability. These dynamic generalizations reveal the possibility for a new scaling dimension complementing that of traditional scaling laws: the number of residual streams, d . Follow-up works have already implemented \ as the default backbone due to its superior modeling capabilities [citation].

In order to further explore the feasibility of this scaling dimension, two works have since introduced the use of exact parameterizations to prevent error accumulation across layers [citation]. These methods additionally avoid the overhead introduced by iterative normalization and overcome the need for custom infrastructure optimization. 
 -lite [citation] invokes the Birkhoff--von Neumann theorem to parameterize ^res as a convex combination of all d! permutation matrices.
However, the (d d!) parameter count explodes super-exponentially: at d=8 , 8!=40,320 permutations are required, rendering this approach memory-prohibitive without sampling approximations that restore an approximation gap.
Conversely, KromHC [citation] uses the Kronecker products of small doubly stochastic matrices to achieve exact constraints at (d^2) complexity, but in doing so, severely trades off expresivity.

We propose a complementary solution rooted in the algebraic theory of generalized orthostochastic matrices [citation].
Our framework for exactly learning doubly stochastic matrices attempts to balance the need for efficiency and expressivity, while exposing a hyperparameter s that allows for tunability across these attributes. This opens up the possibility for further exploration and new approaches towards scaling Hyper-Connections.

In this work, we focus on \ as an example application, but our generalized orthostochastic construction (go- ) can be applied more broadly as a way to parameterize and learn the interior of the Birkhoff polytope. We characterize our parameterization in the context of a synthetic data task mixing d residual streams and a 30M parameter GPT-style language model.

We specifically contribute analytics and explorations that indicate that our method:

Achieves faster convergence and better reachability than parametrizations invoking the Birkhoff von-neumann theorem, as in -lite and KromHC

Scales as (d^3) with the number of residual streams d , both in FLOP complexity and parameter count, surpassing the super-exponential scaling of -lite and KromHC's component factors.

Requires no custom CUDA kernels, relying only on batched linear solves and tensor contractions.

Achieves exact double stochasticity by construction, with no iterations and no approximation gap.

Includes a hyperparameter s , which serves as a tunable degree of freedom, interpolating between the orthostochastic boundary ( s=1 ) and the dense interior of the Birkhoff polytope ( s ).

We believe that this method presents a potential avenue for scaling Hyper-Connections with tractable complexity while maintaining significant expressivity. Additionally, our method can be used simultaneously with KromHC to allow for larger Kronecker factors due to the mitigation of the super-exponential scaling introduced in -lite. 

Related Work

Residual connections and their generalizations.
He et al.\ [citation] introduced skip connections that enable gradient flow via identity shortcuts in ResNets.
This paradigm has evolved to become a key component in the design of large language models (LLMs) owing to its stability and scalability [citation].
DenseNet [citation], FractalNet [citation], and subsequent works explored richer connectivity patterns passing intermediate features across networks. Other works have explored novel ways of increasing the effective size of the residual stream [citation], examples include: ResiDual [citation], which explored improvements to layer normalization by doubling the size of the residual stream, and AltUp [citation], which widened the effective hidden dimension while maintaining low compute by processing partitioned representations in different layers.

Hyper-Connections (HC) [citation] proposed the first learnable, multi-stream generalization of residual streams allowing for dynamic mixing matrices. A sketch of one such residual block is shown in the green box of figure~[ref]. HC can theoretically enable models to learn depth and width connections to dynamically rearrange layer connectivity and potentially adds a new avenue for scaling.
Multiple approaches have expanded on this work since, such as splitting
the feature dimension into independent sub-streams [citation] and applying HC to vision and graph neural networks [citation].

However, the unconstrained dynamic connectivity of residual blocks in HC removes the explicit guarantee of the identity property -- introducing the risk of gradient instability after repeated applications of the mixing terms [citation]. This instability impacts deep networks and restricts scalability due to vanishing and exploding gradients. Subsequent works investigate constraining this connectivity to a manifold that bounds gradient updates through the spectral norm in order to address this instability.

Manifold-Constrained Hyper-Connections.
Building on top of Hyper-Connections, Manifold-Constrained Hyper Connections ( ) constrain the dynamic residual matrices to a manifold to improve training stability while still allowing for dynamic connections [citation]. \ primarily changes the definition of _l^res such that it converges to the Birkhoff polytope B_d , where B_d denotes the closed set of doubly stochastic matrices, defined as positive matrices with the following property:

While \ attempts to enforce this constraint after weight updates using the Sinkhorn-Knopp (SK) algorithm [citation], an iterative normalization method for generating doubly stochastic matrices, recent works [citation] aim to build HC networks where the learned map is exactly constrained to the polytope. This has two major implications: 1. implementing the SK kernel requires careful kernel-level optimization for efficiency, and 2. finite SK iterations introduce an approximation gap that accumulates with network depth.

 -lite [citation] attempts to circumvent the approximation gap and customized kernel by making use of the Birkhoff--von Neumann theorem to define the learned connections as a convex combination of the d! permutation matrices (where d is the number of residual streams), at an (dC d!) cost. This method works well for small d , however, this is not tenable in the limit of scaling d > 8 . KromHC [citation] sacrifices expressivity and complete coverage of B_d by using tensor products to train residual connections on a much smaller set of exactly double-stochastic matrices with the added benefit of costing only (d^2 C) .

Cayley transforms in neural networks.

The Cayley transform has been used to parameterize orthogonal matrices in recurrent networks [citation], and comparisons with the matrix exponential show similar quality and better stability at lower FLOP cost [citation].
Li et al. ([citation]) further demonstrate that iterative Cayley-based optimization outperforms matrix exponential approaches for Stiefel manifold problems.

Doubly Stochastic \& Orthostochastic matrices.

Multiple works have investigated the use of doubly stochastic matrices in the context of neural network parameterization [citation]. [citation] introduced generalized orthostochastic matrices, and more recently, [citation] proved that the set of generalized s -orthostochastic matrices is contained in _d and spans all of _d as s . Other works have investigated further properties of the spectral span of the Birkhoff polytope [citation] and the geometry of s -orthostochastic matrices in the quantum setting [citation].
These theoretical foundations underlie our choice of parameterization.
To our knowledge, we are the first to apply this construction in a machine learning setting and, more specifically, to learned residual stream routing in deep networks.

The eigenvalue distribution of d d s -orthostochastic matrices is fundamentally constrained by their membership in the Birkhoff polytope B_d and residence within the Karpelevic region. While 1 -orthostochastic matrices exhibit a restricted, hypo-cycloidal boundary, increasing the parameter s provides a progressively denser approximation of the full B_d [citation]. Consequently, as s increases, the spectral support of s -orthostochastic matrices expands to fill the Karpelevic region, approaching the density of general doubly stochastic matrices, as demonstrated in figure~[ref].

The Spectral Limitation of Kronecker Products

While KromHC is the most efficient of the proposed methods, it imposes a symmetry on the d -streams that significantly prunes the space of potential mixing terms in ^res , as shown in figure~[ref]c. We demonstrate that KromHC parameterizes only a sparse subset of B_d for d>2 by analyzing the spectral reach of k -fold Kronecker products. Specifically, we prove that the spectral space of a k -fold Kronecker product of doubly stochastic matrices is invariant to the index k , effectively restricting the expressive reach of the resulting constraint to that of its constituent base manifold. A formal proof of this invariance is provided in Appendix~[ref].

To illustrate this expressivity bottleneck, consider the 4 4 case where A, B B_2 . Their individual spectra are defined as \1, _A\ and \1, _B\ for [-1, 1] . The spectrum of the Kronecker product A B is thus \1, _A, _B, _A _B\ . Crucially, since the product _A _B remains real and within the interval [-1, 1] , the resulting eigenvalues are strictly contained within the spectral hull of B_2 (see Figure~[ref]b for emperical results and a full proof in [ref]). Due to this closure, the spectral reach of the Kronecker-parameterized manifold is invariant to the number of products and fails to expand into the broader Karpelevic region available to the full Birkhoff polytope, representing increasingly smaller fractions of the full polytope. By volume, this is a negligible portion of the full B_d when d > i_k , and we show emperically in appendix~[ref] that this translates to a (d) additional error in the minimum achievable loss compared to -lite and go- .

The collapse in spectral volume for Kronecker-based transforms represents a significant loss in functional diversity. Specifically, these constraints fail to represent cyclic permutations of order greater than i_k=2 (e.g., 1 2 3 1 ), effectively preventing the model from learning complex periodic dependencies. Furthermore, the restriction to real eigenvalues in the 2 2 base case implies that the system can only model pure diffusion (information spreading) without advection (directed rotation of information), severely limiting the directional flow of signals within the reservoir.

FLOP Complexity

We additionally track the additional overhead of our method on top of Hyper-Connections. In unconstrained HC, experiments show negligible incurred FLOP complexity ( 0.15\% increase) during a forward pass in a 7B parameter model [citation]. Projecting onto a manifold with P_M incurs an additional FLOP complexity due to the procedure introduced by M . In , this is significant ( 8\% additional time overhead for a 27B model) even after the development of custom kernels and major infrastructure improvements to perform the Sinkhorn-Knopp iterations. KromHC and -lite (in the small d regime) both show significant speed ups due to the removal of the iterative updates. We here calculate the FLOP complexity of our method to show that it is superexponentially better than -lite and incurs a small relative overhead compared to KromHC for the increased expressivity. For example, for d=16 , the flop complexity of go- \ is 2 orders of magnitude slower than KromHC, compared to the 16 orders of magnitude imposed by -lite. It is also approximately the same order of magnitude in complexity as \ with SK iterations due to (d) (S) .

In general, the model performance improvement is larger than the incurred computational
cost; however, when considering scaling the number of residual streams to allow for richer

middle ground method that provides a good balance of incurred FLOP cost while maintaining

Convergence

The performance and convergence properties of _M are fundamentally governed by the geometry of the underlying loss landscape introduced by M and the stability of the associated gradient signals. In the context of \, this indicates that any target matrix should be reachable regardless of initial conditions using finite optimization iterations. We do expect go- \ to have learnable gradients, be convergent and fully connected (able to get to any target matrix). See appendix [ref] for more details on this claim and associated proofs.

Not all matrix parameterizations are learnable and fully connected. -lite should generally perform well by choosing a good initialization, but we suspect that this does not mitigate the fact that it may become very difficult to relearn an n -stream transform after having previously converged as the model trains for longer due to the potential of vanishing gradients (a heuristic example is from needing to remap streams due to generalization). Numerics and further discussion can be found in the appendix [ref].

Validating with Tiny Language Model

We validate our observations in a tiny language models by comparing the convergence of
go- \ with that of -lite and KromHC in a 30M parameter model with 6 layers and 6 heads in nanoGPT [citation]. We demonstrated similar performance using any of -lite, KromHC and go- \ on TinyStories and Character-level datasets, shown in figure~[ref]. We also ran our method with d=8 (as shown in [ref]), a stream size that would be prohibitively large to run with -lite due to the factorial scaling of its parameter count and complexity as shown in figure~[ref]. This validates our method in principle, and demonstrates it's potential scalability over -lite, but requires additional verification in larger models to more rigourously evaluate and compare the methods. 

We evaluate go- \ against baselines using the LLM-as-a-Judge framework with GPT-4.1, to grade the grammar, creativity, and consistency of stories generated by the models. Our evaluation results are listed in table~[ref]. We generate 70 stories from each model with consensus of 3 independent trials. We use the independent trials to calculate the intraclass correlation coefficient, where we see fairly good reliability. We tested smaller variants of GPT-4.1 and inter-model reliability in appendix~[ref].

Our method achieves parity with established baselines, suggesting our method scales from our toy model as intended. Due to compute limitations, we restrict our experiments to a tiny model, however, prior work [citation] suggests that model performance improves as the number of residual streams is scaled, so we instead attempt to demonstrate that our model achieves equivalent performance to prior work without the ability to claim advantage outside of the small model scenario. Discussion

In this work, we introduced an exact parameterization of the Birkhoff polytope using generalized orthostochastic matrices and demonstrated its application to manifold-constrained hyper-connections. By leveraging the algebraic properties of generalized orthostochastic matrices, go- \ addresses a long-standing trade-off between computational scalability and expressive reach in parameterizing doubly stochastic matrices. Our spectral analysis and loss benchmarks demonstrate that go- \ significantly outperforms existing exact methods like KromHC by filling the Karpelevic region more substantially. This allows the model to capture a broader range of mixing dynamics and maintain expressivity as the residual stream is scaled. Furthermore, our empirical results on synthetic tasks---showing up to a 10 speedup in convergence---suggest that the orthogonal matrix parameterized path within B_d provides a more favorable optimization landscape than the convex combinations used in -lite and KromHC. This efficiency, combined with (d^3) scaling, provides a viable technical path for increasing the number of residual streams as a new scaling dimension for deep networks.

Despite these advantages, our approach has several limitations. Our experiments were primarily conducted with synthetic tasks and scale-limited language models. While these results establish the method's correctness and relative advantages, the degree to which these convergence benefits translate to real-world performance remains an open question. Additionally, while (d^3) complexity is a significant improvement over -lite’s factorial scaling, it introduces a nontrivial overhead compared to KromHC’s (d^2) cost for large d . Using go- \ in tandem with Kronecker-factored methods remains a promising strategy for bridging this complexity gap while maintaining expressivity. Finally, the dependence on the Cayley transform introduces a matrix inversion bottleneck; future work could explore alternative mappings to the orthogonal group, such as Householder reflections or Hurwitz angles, to further optimize hardware-level efficiency.

The Orthostochastic--Unistochastic Gap

 

 While we used orthogonal matrices in go- , we in principle could have also used unitary matrices with complex entries instead. We conjecture that the implications on the geometry and expressivity are minimal when s 2 , and that the convergence is of similar order of magnitude, although it might exhibit a different curvature depending on the position and velocity vectors within the manifold.
 
 If expressivity is reduced, we expect that when sampling matrices, we get worse overall performance. We see in the 3 examples from figure~[ref] that the total achievable loss is similar for unistochastic and doubly stochastic matrices for d \3, 6\, s=2, \10^-4, 10^-2\, _p \0, 1\ , regardless of the target matrix location. Plot~[ref](a) targets a matrix at the boundary of the polytope (specifically, the average of two permutation matrices) and Plot~[ref](b-c) target a random matrix sampled from (0, 1) with Sinkhorn-Knopp applied on it.
 
 Since the boundaries of the sets of orthostochastic and unistochastic matrices are the
 same, we additionally expect that they are both equally able to express the boundary of the polytope, except for the additional relative phases that need to cancel out in the unitary matrices. We see this manifest as slower convergence for the unitaries.

 On the other hand, for a random point within the polytope, there are many more solutions of phases
 that satisfy the target matrix condition, and therefore, we see faster convergence. We think this is due to being able to go to a point that is overall closer to the initialization due to the phase terms allowing for interference.

Model, Hyperparameters \& Training Details

 

 We trained a 6-layer Transformer-based language model on the TinyStories dataset using a modified version of the nanoGPT codebase [citation]. 

 The model follows the decoder-only transformer architecture, specifically configured for the TinyStories synthetic dataset. The model consists of 6 layers with an embedding dimension of d_model = 384 . Each layer utilizes a multi-head attention mechanism with h=6 heads, resulting in a per-head dimension of d_k = d_v = 64 . The position-wise feed-forward networks (FFN) employ a hidden dimensionality of d_ff = 4d_model = 1536 .

 Our model utilizes the standard GPT-2 tokenizer with a vocabulary size of V = 50,257 . The embedding layer, W_e R^V d_model , accounts for approximately two thirds of our parameters (20M of the 30M total parameters). We did not test smaller embedding matrices, but this would be interesting to do given the simpler vocabulary of TinyStories.
 
 Training was performed using the AdamW optimizer with a peak learning rate of 1 10^-3 , decaying to 1 10^-4 via a cosine schedule over 20,000 iterations. To ensure training stability, we employed a linear warmup of 500 steps. We utilized an effective batch size of 128 sequences, achieved through gradient accumulation on a single NVIDIA RTX 4090 GPU using bfloat16 mixed-precision training. Weighted-Average of Doubly-Stochastic Matrices

 

 We discuss two alternative formulations one can use for parameterizing doubly-stochastic matrices. We add an additional s learned parameters \ _i i \1, ,s\\ and instead weighted average a set of s d d orthostochastic matrices using the weights _i .

 

Application to Orthostochastic\&Unistochastic Matrices

 

 While we use the d^2 s s blocks in our construction to build the class of matrices, this scales as (s^3d^3) . We have found that an alternative formulation here where a transition matrix can be described as the sum of Orthostochastic (or Unistochastic) matrices to be effective at filling the Birkhoff polytope. This scales as (sd^3) instead of (s^3d^3) and fills the Birkhoff polytope in a different manner. Any Doubly Stochastic matrix, in the limit of multiple sums, can be represented as the sum of other doubly stochastic matrices. While the Karpelevic region is filled entirely almost immediately (see figure~[ref]), this prioritizes filling the facets of the Birkhoff polytope first (hence the Karpelevic region being filled entirely). This method however leaves substantial gaps while filling the facets and therefore we opt to use the method in the main text. Further exploration of this would be interesting as a potentially lower-complexity way of learning the manifold.

 
 
 

Application to KromHC

 

 KromHC introduces an inherent symmetry, we can attempt to break the symmetry by introducing weighted sums similar to section~[ref] and summing the outcomes of indivdual KromHC forward passes. Emperically, we see that we need n^2/ (n)$ terms to achieve full expressivity, which takes us close to the limit of performance in go- .
