# AI-generated equivalent of: Steerable Visual Representations
# Original arXiv: 2604.02327

To evaluate the efficacy of steerable representations, we constructed a rigorous evaluation protocol centered on dynamic feature modulation. Our benchmark, CORE (COnditional REtrieval), assesses whether visual representations can be dynamically reconfigured through linguistic prompts without requiring task-specific fine-tuning. We selected six distinct scene categories from SUN397—specifically, three indoor environments (kitchen, living room, bedroom) and three outdoor contexts (beach, forest, mountain trail)—and generated 100 base images per category. For each scene-object pairing, we employed FLUX.2 to inpaint five semantically appropriate objects (e.g., "ceramic vase" in living rooms, "hiking boots" on trails), yielding 3,000 manipulated images alongside their unaltered counterparts. This design isolates the steering capability: models must retrieve images based on specific object mentions while ignoring distractors sharing the same scene context.

We evaluate using mean Average Precision (mAP) at threshold 0.5 and a novel Steerability Coefficient (SC), defined as the cosine similarity divergence between query-specific and query-agnostic feature vectors normalized by the text embedding magnitude. This metric quantifies how effectively text prompts redirect the visual embedding space. All models process images at 336×336 resolution; for architectures with fixed input pipelines, we bicubically resize maintaining aspect ratio through center cropping.

Quantitative analysis reveals substantial disparities in steerability across architectural families. Unimodal encoders (DINOv2, MAE) exhibit near-random performance on CORE (mAP 12.3% and 14.7% respectively), confirming that self-supervised pretraining produces invariant features incapable of dynamic linguistic modulation. Cross-modal encoders employing late fusion (CLIP, SigLIP) demonstrate moderate retrieval capabilities (mAP 34.8% and 38.2%), yet their element-wise addition of text embeddings fails to fundamentally restructure visual representations, resulting in high false-positive rates when queries specify fine-grained attributes. 

Multimodal Large Language Models (InternVL3, Qwen3-VL, LFM-2.5-VL) show improved steering (mAP ranging 41.2%–46.5%) through last-token pooling strategies, though their performance plateaus on compound queries requiring simultaneous attribute binding (e.g., "red fruit bowl" vs. "ceramic fruit bowl"). Open-vocabulary localization models (SAM3, GroundingDINO) achieve high precision on spatial grounding but underperform in global scene retrieval (mAP 28.4% and 31.6%), as their intermediate representations prioritize local pixel correspondence over holistic semantic composition.

Our proposed steerable representation architecture significantly outperforms all baselines, achieving 67.3% mAP on CORE and an SC of 0.84. Crucially, our model demonstrates compositional generalization: when evaluated on novel object-scene combinations absent during training (e.g., "telescope" inpainted into kitchens), performance degrades gracefully (mAP 54.1%) compared to the catastrophic failure of late-fusion approaches (mAP <20%). 

These results illuminate fundamental mechanisms underlying representational steerability. The efficacy gap between early and late fusion suggests that text conditioning must operate at multiple hierarchical levels rather than merely biasing final layer activations. Ablations indicate that removing cross-attention mechanisms in intermediate layers reduces SC by 40%, implicating deep architectural integration as essential for dynamic modulation. Furthermore, analysis of failure modes reveals that steerability diminishes when text queries contain negation or disjunction ("kitchen without fruit bowl"), suggesting current architectures struggle with logical composition in feature space.

The computational overhead of steerable representations remains modest—our approach requires 1.3× the FLOPs of static encoders but 0.7× compared to full MLLM forward passes. This efficiency profile positions steerable representations as viable drop-in replacements for retrieval systems requiring dynamic vocabulary support. Future work must address the temporal persistence of steering: current representations require continuous text conditioning, whereas persistent modulation through memory mechanisms could enable more efficient batched retrieval across varying query contexts.
