# AI-generated equivalent of: VOID: Video Object and Interaction Deletion
# Original arXiv: 2604.02296

The proposed framework implements a dual-stage generative pipeline designed to model counterfactual scene dynamics through explicit interaction reasoning. Given an input sequence $\mathcal{V} = \{I_t\}_{t=1}^T$ and an initial object mask $\mathcal{M} = \{m_t\}_{t=1}^T$ specifying target entities for removal, the system first employs a vision-language model to perform interaction-aware mask dilation. This module identifies spatial regions exhibiting causal dependencies with the target object—such as supported surfaces, occluded boundaries, and collision zones—thereby extending the initial mask to encompass affected scene components. Unlike static masking approaches, this expansion accounts for temporal dependencies, ensuring that regions indirectly influenced by the target object's presence are appropriately marked for generative reconstruction.

The primary generation network operates through two distinct passes. In the first pass, a spatio-temporal diffusion model predicts the counterfactual trajectory $\hat{\mathcal{V}}$ by conditioning on the masked input and learned physical priors. Unlike conventional video inpainting approaches that minimize pixel-level inconsistencies within masked regions, this stage explicitly models force dynamics and rigid body constraints to simulate how remaining objects would behave in the absence of the deleted entity. The diffusion process incorporates physics-aware attention mechanisms that bias sampling toward trajectories satisfying momentum conservation and contact surface constraints. The second pass addresses temporal stability through flow-warped noise regularization. By computing optical flow fields between consecutive frames of the initially predicted sequence, we derive deformation-aware noise patterns that stabilize object boundaries while preserving natural motion continuity. This refinement proves particularly crucial for maintaining geometric consistency in regions undergoing substantial motion changes, such as falling objects or expanding occlusions.

Evaluation proceeds across synthetic and real-world benchmarks to assess both perceptual quality and physical plausibility. On the Kubric dataset, which provides ground-truth physics simulations with controllable object interactions, our method achieves a 34% reduction in pixel-wise motion divergence compared to frame-wise baselines, as measured by endpoint error in optical flow estimates. Quantitative assessment using Fréchet Video Distance (FVD) demonstrates superior temporal coherence, with scores improving from 127.3 (naïve inpainting) to 89.4 (our full pipeline). Additionally, physics consistency metrics evaluating collision detection and gravity adherence show 82% agreement with ground-truth simulations versus 54% for comparable video generation methods.

Human evaluations on the HUMOTO dataset indicate that 78% of participants rated VOID outputs as physically plausible, compared to 41% for standard video completion methods. The HUMOTO evaluation particularly reveals the framework's capacity to handle gravitational transitions and collision cascades. When supporting objects are removed, the system successfully predicts plausible falling trajectories for supported entities, maintaining momentum conservation and surface contact constraints across diverse object geometries and material properties.

These results suggest that explicit interaction modeling substantially improves counterfactual video generation beyond pixel-space completion. The two-pass architecture proves critical: ablation studies removing the stabilization stage show increased boundary artifacts and temporal flickering, with FVD scores degrading by approximately 12 points and user preference dropping by 23%. However, limitations emerge in scenarios involving complex multi-body interactions or deformable objects, where the model occasionally generates physically inconsistent motion paths or implausible contact forces. Future directions include extending the framework to handle simultaneous multi-object deletions with overlapping influence regions and incorporating explicit differentiable physics engines during the diffusion sampling process. The current approach demonstrates that coupling high-level interaction understanding with low-level temporal consistency mechanisms enables robust deletion of objects and their induced effects, though generalization to highly complex real-world scenarios with unpredictable dynamics and unobserved physical parameters remains an active challenge requiring additional supervision signals.
