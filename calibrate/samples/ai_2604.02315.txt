# AI-generated equivalent of: Beyond the Assistant Turn: User Turn Generation as a Probe of Interaction Awareness in Language Models
# Original arXiv: 2604.02315

To operationalize user-turn generation as a diagnostic probe, we construct a forced-role continuation task wherein the model is explicitly primed to generate under the user identity. Formally, given a conversation trajectory $C = \{u_1, a_1, \ldots, u_n, a_n\}$ where $u_i$ and $a_i$ denote user and assistant utterances respectively, we append a role-indicating prefix "\textsc{User}:" to the context and quantify the subsequent generation $u_{n+1}$ for contextual groundedness. A generation is classified as a genuine follow-up only if it satisfies two criteria: (1) semantic relevance to the specific content of $a_n$ rather than generic conversational templates, and (2) logical temporal ordering consistent with the information state established in the preceding exchange. We operationalize criterion (1) through perturbation sensitivity analysis: we systematically modify $a_n$ to produce $a_n'$ via controlled interventions (answer flipping in mathematical contexts, negation insertion in instruction-following scenarios, and entity substitution in open-domain dialogue) and measure whether the distribution over $u_{n+1}$ shifts commensurately. Models exhibiting interaction awareness demonstrate significant Jensen-Shannon divergence between $P(u_{n+1} \mid C)$ and $P(u_{n+1} \mid C')$ under these perturbations, whereas models lacking such awareness generate static, context-independent user queries regardless of assistant content.

Our empirical investigation across eleven open-weight architectures reveals a striking dissociation between task proficiency and interaction awareness. Under deterministic decoding (temperature $T=0$), models achieving state-of-the-art accuracy on GSM8K (exceeding 95\%) nevertheless generate genuine follow-ups at rates approaching zero, instead producing templatic continuations such as "Thank you for the explanation" or "Can you explain further?" independent of whether the assistant response contained a mathematical proof, an erroneous calculation, or a refusal. This pattern persists across model scales within the Qwen3.5 family, where increasing parameters from 0.8B to 72B yields monotonic improvements in mathematical correctness yet negligible gains in grounded user-turn generation under greedy sampling.

However, stochastic decoding reveals that interaction awareness exists in latent form. When sampling at elevated temperatures ($T \in [0.8, 1.2]$), genuine follow-up rates increase substantially, reaching 22\% in the Qwen3.5-72B configuration while remaining absent in smaller variants. This temperature sensitivity suggests that interaction-aware representations reside in the tail of the output distribution, inaccessible through argmax decoding but recoverable through stochastic exploration. Perturbation experiments confirm the validity of our probe: when we adversarially alter assistant responses to introduce factual errors or logical contradictions, high-temperature user-turn generations from large models exhibit corresponding semantic shifts (e.g., expressing confusion or requesting clarification), whereas small models or greedy-sampled large models maintain invariant outputs.

These findings indicate that current post-training paradigms optimize for static correctness within isolated turns while failing to instantiate multi-turn interaction structure in the model's accessible decision boundaries. The near-zero follow-up rates under deterministic conditions demonstrate that interaction awareness, when present, remains epiphenomenal to the training objective rather than constitutive of the model's default behavior. Furthermore, our collaboration-oriented fine-tuning experiments on Qwen3.5-2B—incorporating multi-turn dialogue corpora emphasizing contingent responding—yield measurable increases in grounded follow-up generation, suggesting that interaction awareness is trainable yet neglected by standard instruction-tuning curricula. Consequently, user-turn generation functions not merely as an evaluative metric but as a critical probe of the representational geometry governing dialogical continuity, revealing that high-performance language models may encode sophisticated interactive capabilities that remain sequestered from standard inference modalities.
